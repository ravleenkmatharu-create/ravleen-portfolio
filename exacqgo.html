Overview

The Problem

Johnson Controls' existing access management solutions required dedicated hardware terminals or desktop software. As workplaces evolved toward mobile-first operations, security teams needed a way to manage access on the go — but enterprise-grade security software notoriously prioritizes feature density over usability, leaving non-technical admins frustrated.

The Goal

Design a mobile app that brings the full power of ExacqVision access management to iOS and Android — serving three distinct user types (employees, administrators, and visitors) with a gesture-forward interface that feels as natural as consumer apps while meeting enterprise security requirements.

Context

Working with a Real Industry Partner

This year-long engagement gave me hands-on experience with real enterprise constraints — legal requirements, existing brand systems, technical feasibility reviews, and stakeholder presentations. I collaborated directly with Johnson Controls' product team, navigating feedback cycles and aligning design decisions with business priorities.

Design Process

Enterprise UX at Mobile Scale

The complexity of enterprise access management had to be distilled into a gesture-driven mobile experience. This required deep work on information architecture, permission models, and gesture vocabulary — ensuring power without confusion.

01

Stakeholder Alignment

Conducted discovery sessions with Johnson Controls to map existing workflows, identify pain points, and align on scope and success metrics.

02

Role-Based IA

Designed a permission-aware information architecture that surfaces the right features for each role without overwhelming any user type.

03

Gesture System Design

Developed and documented a consistent gesture vocabulary — swipe, long-press, and pull patterns that map intuitively to security actions like granting access or flagging visitors.

04

Iterative Testing & Handoff

Ran multiple rounds of usability testing, incorporated JCI feedback, and prepared developer-ready specs with a full component library in Figma.

Solution

Key Design Decisions

👆

Gesture-Driven Access Control

Core security actions mapped to natural gestures — swipe to grant entry, long-press to view access history, pull-to-refresh for live door status. Power users can operate the app without reading a single label.

👥

Role-Adaptive Interface

The same app presents three distinct experiences based on user role. Employees see their credentials and access history. Admins get full management controls. Visitors get a streamlined check-in flow — all within one unified codebase.

🔔

Real-Time Security Alerts

Contextual push notifications with progressive disclosure — a brief alert for routine events, detailed drill-down for incidents requiring immediate action. Designed to inform without overwhelming.

Reflection

What I Learned

A year-long industry collaboration is fundamentally different from academic projects. Stakeholder alignment, negotiating scope, presenting design rationale to non-designers, and working within an existing brand system are all skills that only come from real experience. The biggest lesson: constraints from real clients aren't obstacles — they're the design brief. Learning to work creatively within them, and to advocate confidently for user-centered decisions in a business context, was the most valuable thing I took away from ExacqGo.
