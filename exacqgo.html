Enterprise Gesture UX Mobile-First Collaboration

Johnson Controls — ExacqGo

Mobile-first, gesture-driven app for managing secure access across employees, admins, and visitors. Built in collaboration with Johnson Controls.

Role UX Researcher & Designer
Timeline 9 Months — Fall 2024 to Spring 2025
Team 3 design students + Johnson Controls teams
Platform iOS & Android
Tools Figma · Jira · Confluence

Overview

Johnson Controls is a global leader in smart building technologies, operating in over 150 countries with solutions spanning physical security, energy efficiency, and sustainable infrastructure. Their mission: merge innovation with trust by designing secure, user-centered systems where safety, efficiency, and usability coexist.

The project objective was clear: enhance mobile access within their existing ExacqVision platform to enable secure smartphone-based door access, replacing traditional keycards and fobs with a seamless, gesture-driven experience.

3

distinct user roles designed for: employee, administrator, and visitor — each with different permissions and workflows.

12+

months of iterative design, stakeholder reviews, and usability testing in close collaboration with the Johnson Controls team.

1st

mobile-native access management product in the Exacq ecosystem — a new frontier for the platform.

Competitive Analysis

We analyzed 10+ competitors (Suprema, Kisi, Gatewise, Lenel S2, Brivo, Bosch, HID Global, ButterflyMX, SoloInsight) across affordability, mobile integration, UI quality, and integration capabilities. This revealed a critical gap: most competitors sacrificed usability for feature density or lacked the flexibility enterprise clients needed.

01

Key Competitor Limitations. Lack of customization and scalability for diverse enterprise needs. Heavy hardware dependency reducing flexibility. Fragmented visitor management leading to inconsistent experience and security risks.

02

Emerging Market Demands. Cloud-first architectures enabling remote management and real-time updates. Biometric and touchless access methods driven by post-pandemic hygiene concerns. Streamlined onboarding and visitor workflows to reduce friction.

This synthesis revealed an underutilized quadrant: high mobile credential strength + high integration capability with low competition. ExacqGo was positioned to own this space.

Research Findings

To better understand the real-world challenges users face with access management systems, interviews were conducted with key stakeholders, system integrators, and providers. The goal was to identify pain points, unmet needs, and expectations across different roles—from IT employees to security managers to building managers.

Secondary Research explored current trends in mobile-first access control, user-centered security design, and enterprise authentication. Key findings revealed growing demand for IoT-based solutions driven by the need for energy efficiency, compliance, and data security in user-friendly, adaptable systems.

Primary Research involved in-depth interviews examining how users interact with current systems, identifying pain points around data access and usability, and uncovering expectations for future improvements. Economic stabilization and growth have made investors optimistic about enterprise security solutions, particularly those demonstrating strong, user-centered models.

Key Insights

Through a combination of stakeholder interviews and secondary research, three core problem areas were identified that consistently hindered adoption, usability, and long-term system success.

01

Unreliable Badge and Fob Access. Physical access tools were often slow, inconsistent, or prone to failure. Users experienced delays due to missed scans and frequent badge replacements, creating frustration and downtime.

02

Confusing Visitor Experience. Visitors had no clear instructions or self-service entry options. Admins manually managed guest entry using disconnected tools, creating bottlenecks and security risks.

03

Lack of Trust in System Transparency. Users unsure how systems handled data like camera feeds and location tracking. Low confidence in privacy settings and access event feedback.

From this analysis, we formulated our design question:

"How might we create a mobile access control system that is secure and intuitive for managing physical access across work environments?"

Brainstorming & Ideation

We conducted structured brainstorming using Crazy 8s, How Might We prompts, and Affinity Mapping. The team generated dozens of ideas, which we synthesized into four strategic themes based on recurring user needs.

01

Smart Access. Mobile-based ID, SmartTap unlock, facial and fingerprint authentication, fallback PINs, and integration with digital wallets.

02

Real-Time Insights. Live camera feed before unlocking, activity logs, suspicious activity alerts, and granular permission controls.

03

Visitor Management. Temporary access codes with timers, pre-registration flows, QR-based guest entry, and seamless host notification.

04

User Customization. Role-based access controls, UI personalization, simplified admin control panel, and preference management.

Solution

ExacqGo transforms any smartphone into a secure digital credential. The core idea: eliminate the dependency on physical ID cards and create a fluid, user-centered mobile experience where users store digital credentials in Apple Wallet or Google Wallet, powered by enterprise-grade encryption and cloud-based authorization.

01

Home Dashboard

A centralized screen displaying real-time status of favorite doors—Locked, Unlocked, Restricted, or In Range. Users unlock doors, request access, or take quick actions directly from the dashboard. Add guests to upcoming visitor lists for seamless visitor management.

02

Gesture Unlock

Users unlock doors by pointing their phone at the reader and twisting their wrist, mimicking the motion of turning a key. This natural gesture provides quick, touchless access without needing to open the app or press a button. Intuitive, secure, and delightful.

03

Visitor Flow

Hosts invite guests directly from the app by entering their name, visit time, and access area. Visitors receive an email with details and a temporary digital access card they can add to Apple Wallet or Google Wallet. Upon arrival, they simply tap their phone—no manual check-in needed.

04

Door Access & Real-Time Alerts

Users view real-time status of nearby doors and perform quick actions like unlocking, requesting access, or reporting an issue. Live camera feeds available when configured. Contextual alerts for routine events and detailed drill-downs for incidents requiring action.

Usability Testing

We conducted comprehensive usability testing across multiple methods and user types, spanning three structured rounds with moderated and unmoderated sessions.

88.2

System Usability Scale (SUS) score — well above the industry benchmark of 68, indicating excellent usability.

13/16

Key tasks completed on first attempt. The three partial completions were due to minor wording confusion, quickly addressed.

82%

Users expressed willingness to use the product regularly in real-world settings — validating strong adoption intent.

Testing Methods:

01

Post-Test Surveys (Unmoderated). Participants completed System Usability Scale (SUS) and Single Ease Question (SEQ) to quantify usability and perceived task effort.

02

Cognitive Walkthrough (Team-Based). Internal team ran scenario-based walkthroughs assessing learnability using the PURE (Practical Usability Rating by Experts) model.

03

Expert Review (In-Person). Designers and IT employees conducted heuristic evaluations validating interface logic and accessibility standards.

04

Think-Aloud Protocol (Moderated). Users narrated their thought process while performing key tasks, revealing friction points around labeling, button discoverability, and navigation.
